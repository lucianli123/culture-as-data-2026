# The licensing one-pager

One page, one question: **which route to a corpus is allowed?** Referenced in the Week 4
data conversation; keep it beside you whenever you collect.

## The tiers, from open to off-limits

| Tier | Examples | What you may do |
|---|---|---|
| **CC0 / public domain** | Met and MoMA open access, Project Gutenberg, Pantheon | Use, analyze, share, and republish freely. The safe default. |
| **Academic-use datasets** | MovieLens, IMDb datasets, published Goodreads scrapes | Analyze and publish findings; **do not redistribute the data itself**. Link to the source instead. |
| **Lyrics, review text, chart data** | Genius, Rotten Tomatoes, Billboard | Metadata and counts only. Chart positions, dates, and scores are fine; never republish the full text. |
| **Community-opposed archives** | AO3 and other fan-work archives | A small, attributed sample at most, never a shared dataset. These communities actively oppose datamining; respect that. |
| **Live social firehoses** | Bluesky Jetstream, Reddit's live API | We discuss them; we don't scrape them for redistribution. Webis-TLDR-17 and the Stack Exchange dumps are the clean-license routes to messy social text. |
| **Shadow libraries** | LibGen, Anna's Archive | **Never.** Pirated full-text books are the line the field's $1.5B settlement was about. |

## If you scrape (Route 3, the last resort)

1. Read `robots.txt` and the site's terms of service first.
2. Request slowly; identify yourself in the user agent if asked.
3. Take only what the question needs.
4. Never republish copyrighted text; publish your counts and findings instead.

## The people in your data

Licensing is about what you may take; this is about who gets exposed. For social-media
and fan-work corpora (the Data Biography's "People" prompt, made operational):

- **Paraphrase or aggregate; avoid verbatim quotes.** A quoted post is searchable, and
  search de-anonymizes: your essay can make its author findable years later.
- **No usernames in anything you publish**, even when the post was public.
- **Deleted content stays deleted.** If the author removed it, your archive copy is not
  yours to publish.
- **Distress-adjacent communities get extra distance** (support forums, mental-health
  subreddits): aggregate findings only, and reread prompt 5 of the Data Biography —
  *if they saw your essay, what would they want changed?*

## Two rules that resolve most cases

- **Analyze locally, publish findings, link to sources.** Your essay needs your charts and
  numbers, not the underlying text.
- **When unsure, ask before collecting** — in class, in the one-on-one, or of the data's
  maintainers. An hour of asking beats a retracted project.

Your Data Biography (Week 4) records which tier your corpus sits in and why that's
defensible; its distilled form becomes your essay's corpus note in Week 8.
