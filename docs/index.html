Findings that started as counts

Everything below came from someone counting culture. By Week 10 you'll have done the same on a corpus you chose.

  • In the stage directions of 2,000 film screenplays, women are written to snuggle, giggle, and sob; men to strap, gallop, and kill. And 85% of the screenwriters were men. (The Pudding, 2017)
  • Phrases of distorted thinking, like "I am a failure," rose sharply in millions of Google Books after 2000, to the highest levels in more than a century, and the fight over whether that's real is Week 2's opening debate. (Bollen et al., PNAS 2021)
  • A model trained to recognize science fiction misfiled Pynchon's The Crying of Lot 49, and the mistake taught scholars more about genre than the correct answers did. (Underwood, 2016)
  • ChatGPT labeled political tweets more accurately than trained crowd workers, at under a third of a cent per label, roughly twenty times cheaper. (Gilardi et al., PNAS 2023)
  • An AI-generated portrait, Edmond de Belamy, sold at Christie's for $432,500. Who made it is Week 10's closing argument. (2018)

The four tools

  • Counting: the honest floor, with no notion of meaning.
  • Classification: train a transparent reader and see what it learned.
  • Embeddings: items become positions on a map of meaning. The heart of the course.
  • AI annotation: a powerful model reads your whole corpus; you decide which labels to trust.

Images count too. You count, classify, and embed pictures the same way as text.

What you make

  • A reproducible notebook: the record of your analysis, runnable top to bottom.
  • A web essay built on it: an opening question, a few charts, an image finding, a corpus note, about 800 to 1,500 words.

Who it's for

Curious adults, mixed coding backgrounds, most with little or none. The AI writes code; you think about culture.
