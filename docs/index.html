Culture as Data

Ask your own questions about the stories, lyrics, art, and online communities you care about.

Investigate cultural data at scale with an AI as your coding partner. You publish a web essay on a question only you would ask. No mathematics or programming background is required, and the course is free to take.

The four tools

  • Counting: the simplest tool; it has no notion of meaning.
  • Classification: train a transparent reader and see what it learned.
  • Embeddings: items become positions on a map of meaning. The heart of the course.
  • AI annotation: a powerful model reads your whole corpus; you decide which labels to trust.

Images receive the same treatment: you count, classify, and embed pictures as well as text.

The ten weeks

Act 1 — The tools (Weeks 1–3)
Week 1Your First Investigation — Counting: rows, words, pixels
Week 2Counting, Compared: Is the Difference Real? — Advanced counting: baselines, keyness, and the shuffle test
Week 3Classification: Counting with Weights — Classification. Train a reader and read what it learned
Act 2 — The project (Weeks 4–7)
Week 4Pick a Corpus. Pick Two Methods. Commit. — The project (no new mechanism this week)
Week 5Embeddings: A Map of Meaning — Embeddings, the heart of the course, the leap past counting
Week 6Deepen the Project: Images, and Where the AI Fails — Apply the tools harder; image projects' deepest day; calibrate the AI on your data
Week 7The AI as a Reader, at Scale — LLM-as-annotator, the most common operation in the field
Act 3 — Publish (Weeks 8–10)
Week 8Models as Time Capsules + Settle the Finding — What a trained model is: a compression of its corpus
Week 9Build the Page — Publishing, Quarto to GitHub Pages, static by default
Week 10The Showcase — A public celebration; the two capstone assessments

What you make

  • A reproducible notebook: the record of your analysis, runnable top to bottom.
  • A web essay built on it: an opening question, a few charts, an image finding, a corpus note, about 800 to 1,500 words.

Who it's for

Curious adults with mixed coding backgrounds, most with little or none. The AI writes the code; you supply the questions and the judgment.
