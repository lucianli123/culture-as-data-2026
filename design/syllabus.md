# Culture as Data
## Ask Your Own Questions About the Stories, Lyrics, Art, and Online Communities You Love

*A 10-week project-based course for adults. Some coding interest helps; none required. By the end, you'll publish an interactive web essay analyzing a body of cultural data you care about — songs, recipes, museum objects, obituaries, fan-fiction tags, social-media posts — with an AI assistant as your coding partner.*

---

## About This Course

A workshop in **computational cultural analysis** — using code, statistics, and AI to investigate cultural data at scale. You'll learn the methods that researchers and data journalists use to ask large questions of cultural materials. You'll work with real datasets. You'll write code with an AI as your collaborator. And you'll publish an interactive web essay on a question only you would think to ask.

The course is built around **examples**, and around reading them critically. Every week opens with "**Look at This**" — a finished study or *Pudding*-style piece that did something surprising with the week's method — so you see destinations before you start journeys, and it grows into a real discussion (one of the three things every session balances; see below). But you don't just admire them; you take them apart. A famous paper and a viral data essay aren't truth handed down — they're arguments built on choices about what to count, what to leave out, where to draw the line, choices that are sometimes wrong. Every week you'll ask of the featured work: *what did they choose, what could they have done differently, where might this be wrong?* By the end you'll read this work the way its authors' peers do — admiration and skepticism at once — and see the same kind of choices in your own project.

**And the course gives you real tools, not just counting.** You'll learn four ways to turn culture into something a machine can analyze, each one expanding what you can do with your own material past the one before: **counting** (powerful, but it has no notion of meaning), **classification** (train the machine to sort your corpus and read what it learned), **embeddings** (the heart of the course — turn each item into a position on a map of meaning, so similar things land near each other and patterns appear that counting can't reach), and **using an AI to read your whole corpus at scale** — handing the model thousands of items and having it judge each one, then learning to trust the labels it's sure of and check the ones it isn't. The signature move is taking a question you first asked by counting and answering it again, more richly, by meaning. Images are a full part of this: you count, classify, and embed pictures exactly as you do text, so a project on album covers or paintings is as well-supported as one on lyrics. No math; everything is hands-on and visual. And the systems reading culture at scale stop being black boxes — the feeds choosing your next song, the models trained on our books and art, the AI writing your code, which this course treats as a tool, not a subject.

**Social media counts as cultural data.** Half of the Week 2 corpus tour features social-media corpora (Bluesky, Reddit, Letterboxd). The course's corpus library is frank about what's accessible in the post-API era and the public-but-not-public ethics that come with it.

**This is project-based, not a survey.** You'll commit to one corpus and one question by Week 4 and spend the rest of the term making something publishable about it.

## The Shape of the Course

**Every session balances three things, roughly a third each:** **lecture/demo** (an instructor walks through the week's tool and writes code live), **workshop** (you build hands-on, alone or with a partner), and **discussion** (the room digs into a study, debates an interpretation, or gives feedback on each other's projects). Some weeks tilt — Week 1 is mostly demo and building, Week 10 is almost all discussion at the showcase — but the default is an even split, so you're never just listening, just building, or just talking.

**Weeks 1–3: Tour.** Shared toy datasets, text and image; the first rungs of the ladder (counting, then classification); all four project methods previewed before you commit. No commitments yet — lots of trying.

**Weeks 4–7: Project.** Commit to a corpus and your methods. Each week balances a tool block, hands-on project work, and a real discussion — embeddings, the heart of the course (W5); deeper application and images (W6); the AI as a reader at scale (W7) — with the discussion often a debate (is a pattern found in a corpus a discovery about culture, or its own bias read back?) or peer critique of each other's findings. **If your project stalls** — a corpus that won't cooperate, a question with no findable answer — that's expected, not failure: there's a bank of pre-tested fallback corpus-and-question pairs you can adopt and still finish, and a null result honestly shown ("I expected X, the data doesn't show it, here's how I know") is a complete project. The mid-course one-on-ones (Weeks 5–6) are where we sort this out if it happens. And the pace has give built in — if the cohort needs to catch up, we compress the lighter material, never the core.

**Weeks 8–10: Publish.** Three weeks scaffolding the deliverable. Week 8 is final analysis and prose drafting. Week 9 is the build session — you leave with a deployed URL. Week 10 is the public showcase with snacks and friends.

**Mid-course one-on-ones run during Weeks 5 and 6** — 20 minutes per student, outside class. Is your project on track? Is your method working? Do we need to swap something out?

**Time outside class:** Plan ~3 hours per week. Less in Weeks 1–3 and 10; more in Weeks 4–9.

**If your project is failing:** This will happen to someone. APIs change, corpora turn out smaller than they looked, methods produce nothing legible. The instructor maintains a starter corpora list any student can adopt — spanning genre fiction, advice and argument forums, song lyrics over time, image sets, and structured records — so a pivot stays inside what you came to study. Pivoting is part of the work. Tell the instructor early.

**Accessibility:** The course assumes vision (for image analysis), keyboard use (for coding), and English fluency (for readings). If any is a barrier, talk to the instructor before Week 1 — most of the course adapts.

## Eight Things You'll Be Able to Do

The published essay is the thing you'll *show* people. But it's not the point. The point is what you'll be able to do afterward, with or without an AI in the room. By Week 10 you should be able to:

1. **Read a block of analysis code and say what it does** — line by line, in your own words, without running it.
2. **Ask a tractable question of a pile of cultural data** — one that's specific enough to actually answer, and honest about what it leaves out.
3. **Name what a method assumes and what it hides.** Every method makes the world legible by throwing some of it away. You'll be able to say what got thrown away.
4. **Read an embedding map, a classifier's labels, or a character network skeptically** — tell the difference between a real pattern and a pretty accident.
5. **Judge what these tools can and can't show, and the ethics of the data behind them** — explain in plain language what counting, classification, embeddings, and AI annotation each reveal and hide; read an AI's confidence to know which results to trust; and reckon with the fact that the models reading your corpus learned their judgment from creative work scraped largely without permission.
6. **Write a Data Biography** — account for where a dataset came from, who's in it, who isn't, and what it can't tell you.
7. **Judge AI-generated code instead of just running it** — spot where it's wrong, where it's insecure, where it quietly did something other than what you asked.
8. **Read a published study, data essay, or chart and see the choices behind it** — name the interpretative decisions its authors made (including how a visualization frames its case: axis, scale, what's shown and dropped), where the work could have gone differently, and where it might be wrong. Treat finished work as something people built, not truth handed down — hold admiration and skepticism at once, and turn the same eye on your own charts before you publish.

These eight are the spine; everything in the weekly plan serves one or more, and *How You'll Know You're Learning* (below) explains how each is checked.

## What You'll Make

You'll make two things, kept separate on purpose. The **notebook** is where the analysis lives — the real record of your thinking. The **essay** is how you tell the world what you found.

### 1. A reproducible notebook (the analysis)

A Colab/Jupyter notebook someone else could open and run top to bottom to get your results — the primary evidence that *you* did the analysis rather than commissioned it. It holds your code, data-loading, method, and short prose cells explaining each step. The test, borrowed from Lincoln Mullen: *the instructor must be able to run it.* If it only runs on your machine with your tweaks, it isn't done.

### 2. A web essay (the communication)

A published web essay at a shareable URL, built in Act 3 *on top of* the finished notebook. The shape is a short data-journalism piece in the *Pudding* tradition. Within that shape there's room to be weird. It includes:
- An opening question
- Two to four data visualizations
- At least one image-based finding
- ~800–1,500 words of prose
- A **corpus note** — your Data Biography in distilled form
- A **"show your work" appendix** — your method choices, your moments of doubt, what you cut, what the AI got wrong. This is where you do to your *own* work what we've done to the pros' all term: name the interpretative choices you made and where a reader could reasonably disagree. The honesty that you learned to look for in published work, turned on yourself. (This practice — showing your work rather than presenting clean results — comes from D'Ignazio & Klein's *Data Feminism*, which runs quietly under the whole course.)
- A "what surprised me" closing

**The essay is the communication layer, not the proof you learned anything.** That proof lives in the notebook, the Data Biography, and the oral walkthrough (see *How You'll Know You're Learning*). This is deliberate: it means a beautiful page built on thin analysis won't fool anyone, and a plain page built on real analysis is a success.

**Tooling.** The publishing toolchain (Quarto → GitHub Pages) is pre-built as a template you fork in Week 4, when your project commits, so that by the Act 3 build it's familiar furniture rather than a new thing to learn under deadline. Defaults and opt-ins:
- **Default:** Quarto with static HTML + SVG output, published to GitHub Pages. No JavaScript state. Sufficient for an essay worth reading.
- **Opt-in:** JavaScript interactivity. If you opt in, you accept an additional code-review step in Week 9, because LLM-generated JavaScript carries documented security and accessibility risks.
- **Power-user:** Observable, if you (or the AI) are comfortable with JavaScript. Same review step applies.

**A note about ambition.** *Pudding* pieces are produced by professional designer-engineers over weeks or months. A 10-week course cannot produce them. What it can produce is an essay shaped like one, with one or two of its moves done well rather than five done shallowly. Pick a small question and answer it carefully.

## How You'll Know You're Learning

This course has no grades. That's not the same as no standards. Grades are how institutions sort people; this course isn't sorting anyone, so it doesn't need them. But you and the instructor both need to *know* whether the eight things above are actually forming — and because an AI can produce work you don't understand, a polished result is not enough to tell. So the course checks the thinking directly, often, and in small low-stakes ways.

**Your workbench (a portfolio, not a test).** Across the term you keep a single folder: your notebook, your Data Biography, one thing you revised after feedback, and your weekly check-out notes. At the showcase, a one-paragraph intro to the folder — *here's what I can do now that I couldn't in Week 1* — is the closest thing to a final exam. The criteria are the eight competencies, and you know them from day one.

**Weekly checks, with the AI closed.** Once a week the assistant goes off and you do one small thing by hand — trace what a cell does, explain your last result aloud, fix a deliberately-broken snippet, or **cold-read a study or chart you've never seen and name one choice its authors made that you could argue with**. None of it graded. It exists so the gap between "I can run this" and "I understand this" surfaces early, to you first. (In Week 6 this becomes the term's sharpest version: you hand-label thirty items the AI also labels, then study every disagreement.) The cold-read check is how reading finished work critically — the habit at the center of this course — gets real evidence, on unseen work, where the AI can't help in the moment.

**The oral walkthrough.** Twice — once mid-course, once at the showcase — you walk someone through how you got a result and, just as important, what it does *not* show, and how the tools that got you there actually work. This is the part an AI can't do for you, which is exactly why it's the best evidence you learned something.

**Reading work critically.** Not checked with a quiz — it's the running "Look at This, then Question It" practice, the Week 2 trial of a famous study, and finally your own "show your work" appendix, where you turn that scrutiny on yourself.

**Peer feedback is for learning, not grading.** When classmates respond to your work it's to help you see it fresh, never to score you. The protocols are structured (Weeks 8–9) so feedback stays specific and kind.

## Class Rituals

**Warm-up retrieval (5 min, opens every class).** Before anything new, a couple of quick from-memory questions about earlier weeks — no notes, no AI, not graded. "Why does the model see tokens instead of words?" "Why did we hand-label thirty things before trusting the AI's labels?" Pulling old material out of your head is one of the few study moves with strong evidence behind it, and it keeps Week 2 alive in Week 7 instead of letting each week become an island.

**Look at This, then Question It (5 min).** One finished study or article tied to the week's method: first what's cool, then *what did the authors choose, and what could they have chosen instead?* One arguable decision named each week. (Full rationale under *How You'll Know You're Learning*.)

**Show-and-Tell (5 min).** One student shares a finding or a corpus they encountered. No prep, no slides.

**The Sketch (homework, ungraded).** A 30-minute low-stakes play exercise.

**What Surprised You? (check-out).** A weekly reflection — varies by week, sometimes two sentences, sometimes a paragraph, sometimes a screenshot with commentary. Submitted privately. This is the running record of what the AI got right and wrong on your work.

**Social rules** (borrowed from the Recurse Center programming retreat — norms that make a room safe to be a beginner in):
- **No feigning surprise.** Of course you don't know what a regex is. Nobody is born knowing.
- **No well-actually's.** Pedantic corrections that don't change the conversation.
- **No backseat driving in pair sessions.** Let your partner type.
- **No subtle -isms.** Sexism, racism, ableism — including the casual stuff.

Breaking a rule is normal. We say "oops" and move on.

The rules don't work by being posted. In Week 1 the instructor models each rule by deliberately breaking it and being corrected — including the rule that the instructor isn't exempt. Throughout the term, we say "oops" out loud when we slip. The rules are there to protect the room, not to police anyone.

## Reading

One required reading per week, kept under thirty minutes. One optional supplement. And one *deeper* option each week — the full paper or serious essay behind the session, never required — for those who want the scholarship itself. Required academic papers stay "abstract + figures" reads; the deeper column is where full papers live.

**How to read everything here.** Bring three questions to every reading and featured project, and we'll often open them up in class: *What did the authors decide?* (the choices — what to count, what to exclude, where to draw the line, what to claim). *What's the load-bearing assumption?* (the thing that, if it's wrong, sinks the conclusion). *Where's the gap?* (the step that doesn't quite follow, the alternative reading they didn't address). This isn't about catching people out. The best work in the field is rigorous *and* contestable at once; reading well means holding both. Several of the assigned pieces are scholars doing exactly this to their own field — and to themselves.

## The Data Biography

One of your most important deliverables isn't your essay — it's your **Data Biography**, written in Week 4 when you commit to a corpus. Borrowed from Heather Krause's framework at We All Count, it's a frank account of where your data comes from and what it can and cannot tell you.

A Data Biography answers, at minimum:

1. **Origin** — Who collected this data, when, where, and why? What were they trying to do?
2. **Funding & power** — Who paid for the collection? Whose interests does it serve?
3. **Method** — How was each row in your dataset produced? What had to happen for a record to exist?
4. **Selection** — What is in this dataset that wouldn't be in a different collection of "the same thing"? What's missing?
5. **People** — Who are the humans in this data, and what is their relationship to it? Did they know they would become a row? If they saw your essay, what would they want changed?
6. **Limits** — What questions can this data not answer, no matter how clever the method?

You write it in Week 4 when you commit to your project corpus; its distilled form becomes the corpus note in your final essay. It's the course's ethics backbone — rather than a separate ethics unit, the work of accounting for your data lives here, in the document you build on.

## How to Use the AI

**As a coding partner.** Open a conversation per task. Paste the exact error. Ask "what's the smallest fix?" Always paste code back: "explain this line by line; where would this break?"

**As a thought-partner.** Brainstorm questions, find leads. *Treat every AI-suggested citation as a lead, not a source.* A citation isn't real until you've read the paper.

**As a research scout.** Deep-research tools can survey datasets and prior work in one pass. Use them to find leads before Week 4's commitment; verify every citation and every dataset's access yourself, and never cite the report — cite what you read. The Week 1 deck carries the four rules.

**As an analytical instrument.** For classification, qualitative coding, image captioning. The Week 6 protocol — codebook, prompt, hand-label 30, compare — is the gold standard.

**As a front-end developer.** For Week 9 only, and within limits (see below). Iterate.

**Documenting the AI's role.** Your weekly "What surprised you?" entries are the running record. That is the entire AI-use disclosure.

### What the AI is bad at, and how the course handles it

Recent research (Prather et al. 2024; Kosmyna et al. 2025; the MDPI "fluency illusion" review 2026) documents specific failure modes when novices use LLMs to code:

- **Illusion of competence.** Code that runs is not code that's right. You will feel like you understand work the AI did for you that you could not redo. We design assessments against this.
- **Shifting goalposts.** Students unconsciously scale down their question to match what the LLM can produce. The Week 5 and 6 one-on-ones are partly there to catch this.
- **Security holes in generated code.** Industry tests of AI-generated code find ~45% contains exploitable weaknesses. For this reason, the course's default web-publishing target is static HTML + SVG, with no JavaScript state. If you opt into JavaScript interactivity, you accept an additional review step.
- **Confident wrong on non-English text.** LLMs underperform on low-resource languages by measurable margins. If your corpus is not in English, you must validate annotations against a small hand-labeled sample (the course shows you how).

### The Gemini-free block

Each session includes one ~20-minute stretch with the assistant closed — you write, read, and debug code by hand. Non-negotiable: learners who never work without an AI can't later reproduce their own work, and the goal is that you leave able to do this, not to commission it. This is where the weekly check (trace / explain / fix / cold-read critique) lives — see *How You'll Know You're Learning*.

## Week 2 — Counting, Compared: Is the Difference Real?

**Promise:** Put a famous PNAS paper on trial, build a word-counter by hand, find the words that make a voice distinctive — the method behind Week 1's featured study — and learn the one statistics move this course needs: the shuffle test for whether a difference is real.

**The set-piece — the trial.** The claim: a hockey stick of societal distress, found by counting. The objection: the rising words are fiction-words, and Google scanned more fiction after 2000. The rebuttal — and the move you'll steal for your own work: the authors *removed the entire fiction corpus and re-ran the analysis*, and the pattern largely held. The answer to "your corpus is biased" isn't an argument; it's a test. The books, as the critic himself put it, are a treasure trove *when interpreted with care.*

| 0:25 | **Bag-of-words by hand.** Two authors, highlighters, tally the top words. Argue about merging "run/running." The arguments are the lesson: counting requires defining. |
| 0:50 | **What counts as a word?** Paste the same sentence into two tokenizer playgrounds and watch it shatter differently into colored chips. Models never see words — they see tokens, and *which* tokens is a design choice, the same one you just argued about with run/running. |
| 1:05 | Break |
| 1:15 | **tf-idf.** The AI scales your hand count; stop-words dominate, which motivates tf-idf: "common here, rare overall." (Zipf's law — the eerie straight line hiding in every text — is this week's five-minute supplement to try at home.) |
| 1:30 | **Keyness — the *She Giggles, He Gallops* move.** From "common here" to "distinctively hers": a log-odds ratio between two corpora finds the words one voice uses far more than a baseline — and it's exactly the method behind Week 1's featured piece. ## Week 3 — Classification: Counting with Weights

**Promise:** Watch a machine learn a bias in ten minutes, build a classifier and read its mind — what words it leaned on, where it would fail — and preview the full methods menu before next week's commitment.

**Look at This, then Question It:** Ted Underwood's genre prediction (*Distant Horizons*, 2019) — a classifier trained to recognize detective fiction and science fiction across a century of novels, tracing how those genres took shape. The interrogation: *what counts as science fiction* is a choice built into the training labels — and the model's most famous "error," misreading Pynchon's *The Crying of Lot 49* (a novel critics read as a detective-fiction spoof), shows that genre boundaries are real but fuzzy. A classifier's mistakes can teach you as much as its successes; yours will too.

## Week 5 — Embeddings: A Map of Meaning

**Promise:** This is the heart of the course — your own corpus, text or images, sorts itself by meaning, and the finding counting couldn't give you appears. You also see the same trick is how "For You" feeds place you. (Mid-course one-on-ones begin this week — see *The Shape of the Course*.)

**Look at This, then Question It — a debate** *(from this week on, the room names the choice before the instructor does)*: two readings of the same discovery — that embedding space contains *directions*. **Kozlowski, Taddy & Evans (2019)** find a rich–poor axis and an education axis and read them as the cultural structure of social class, tracking how the meaning of affluence shifted across a century: culture made measurable. **Bolukbasi et al. (2016)** find the same kind of direction — a gender axis pairing men with "programmer" and women with "homemaker" — and read it as prejudice the model absorbed and researchers should remove. Identical technique, opposite verdicts. The question the room has to settle: *when is a dimension found in a corpus a discovery about culture, and when is it the corpus's own bias read back* — and what makes the difference, the data, the purpose, or what you do with the result? (The same tension returns in a second pairing the discussion can draw on: embeddings as bias warning — Caliskan, 2017 — versus embeddings recovering which abolitionist newspapers led the movement's language, crediting two women-edited papers and the Black press — Soni, Klein & Eisenstein, 2021.)

## Week 7 — The AI as a Reader, at Scale

**Promise:** Use the AI to read your whole corpus at once — judging each item the way a human coder would, but in minutes — and learn the skill that makes that trustworthy: knowing which labels to believe and which to check by hand. Plus a working block on your project, and an honest look at whose judgment you're borrowing when an AI reads creative work.

**Look at This, then Question It:** Gilardi, Alizadeh & Kubli (2023) found ChatGPT *outperformed* crowd workers on several text-labeling tasks — cheaper, faster, more consistent. The interrogation (room first): the prompt *is* the codebook (you decide what "sarcastic" means, and a different prompt relabels everything); agreement with humans isn't ground truth; and the model fails *silently* — confident wrong answers a human's disagreement would have caught. And the deeper one: its "judgment" is a compression of its training data, much of it scraped creative work, so whose reading are you renting?

| Time | Activity |

## Week 8 — Models as Time Capsules + Settle the Finding

**Promise:** You meet models that live entirely inside one historical period, learn what a famous model has secretly memorized, kill your own finding to see what survives — and leave with a prose draft.

**Look at This, then Question It — models as time capsules.** A model is a compression of its corpus; bound the corpus and you bound the mind. **TimeCapsuleLLM** is a small GPT trained from scratch on nothing but 1800s London publications — ask it about today and it answers from a Victorian world, because that world is all it contains. Then the inversion, **"Speak, Memory"** (Bamman's lab, 2023): instead of choosing a model's diet, probe what a famous one already ate — tests revealed which books GPT-4 has effectively memorized, heavy on sci-fi, fantasy, and a familiar canon. A "training canon" nobody curated on purpose. The interrogation: a period model speaks only for the period's *published, surviving, digitized* voices — who got printed in 1850s London? — and memorization isn't reading. It also bites back on last week's annotator work: the authors show models score higher on memorized books than unmemorized ones, so an LLM that seems to read your corpus may be partly reciting it — a validity problem, not just an ethics one. This is Week 2's lesson at full scale: the corpus *is* the worldview; bounded deliberately, it becomes the instrument.
